# Inside IRA: The Design Principles Behind Every Villa

When we began designing the IRA Estates villas, the first conversation we had was not about style. It was about what we wanted residents to feel on an ordinary Tuesday morning, three years into living there.

Not on the day they moved in. Not during the peak season when the pool is perfectly lit and the sea breeze is exactly right. On a regular day — when the light is flat, when nothing particular is happening — would the house still feel right?

That question shaped everything.

## Principle 1: The house should know where it is

Every material, every proportion, every spatial decision at IRA Estates references Goa — not as a theme, but as a fact.

- The laterite stone comes from local quarries
- The rooflines respond to monsoon drainage
- The deep verandahs exist because the Goan afternoon demands shade

A house that could be anywhere is a house that is nowhere. IRA Estates villas could only be here.

## Principle 2: Less surface, more space

We resist ornamentation that does not earn its place. Mouldings, columns, decorative ironwork — these are not wrong in themselves, but in the context of a private pool villa in South Goa, they compete with the landscape rather than framing it.

> "The house should be quiet enough that you notice the sky."

## Principle 3: The transition is as important as the destination

The sequence from gate to door to living room to pool deck is not logistical. It is experiential. At IRA Estates, each transition is designed:

- The compression and release of space
- The moment of first seeing the pool
- The way the living room opens entirely to the outside

These are not accidents. They are the design.

## Principle 4: Durability over novelty

We build for thirty years, not three. This means:

- **Materials that age well** — stone, concrete, hardwood
- **Mechanical systems** that are serviceable and replaceable
- **Surfaces that reward maintenance** rather than requiring replacement

We avoid finishes that look perfect on day one and tired by year five. A beautiful house should become more itself over time, not less.

These principles are not unique to IRA Estates. But they are, in our view, increasingly rare — and worth protecting.
