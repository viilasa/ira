We looked at fourteen sites across Goa before we stopped at Benaulim. Some were larger. Several were cheaper. A few had sea views that would have photographed beautifully.

But Benaulim had something the others did not: a particular quality of stillness that we kept returning to, even when the business case pointed elsewhere.

## What we were looking for

The brief, when we wrote it, was deceptively simple: a site that could support eight exceptional private residences within two minutes of a beach that had not yet been overrun. South Goa was the obvious starting point. Within South Goa, the choices narrowed quickly.

Calangute and Baga were never in consideration — the density and noise work against the kind of home we wanted to build. Palolem was beautiful but remote. Colva had the infrastructure but had given too much of itself to mass tourism.

## Benaulim, on its own terms

Benaulim is different. The beach is long and wide and largely uncrowded even in peak season. The village itself has retained a domestic scale — the churches, the lanes, the fishermen's houses still running alongside the newer villas. There is a rhythm to the place that resists the kind of acceleration that has consumed the north.

The soil is good. The laterite is close to the surface. The water table behaves. These things matter for construction quality and for long-term maintenance — considerations that tend to disappear in the excitement of a sea view but return, reliably, once you are living there.

## Why we stayed

The moment we knew was standing on the site at 6pm on a February evening. The light was doing what Goan light does in that hour — turning everything a particular shade of amber, making the palms look like they had been arranged. The sea was audible but not visible. The nearest sound after that was a motorbike, about a kilometre away.

> "We were not looking for perfect. We were looking for right. Benaulim was right."

We broke ground six months later.
