At 6,000 ft, in the pine forests above Chandigarh, IRA Estates is quietly preparing its second chapter.

Kasauli is not a new discovery. The British knew what they were doing when they built their summer retreats here in the 1840s — the elevation, the cool air, the view south across the plains. What has changed is who is coming now, and why.

## Why the hills, why now

The pandemic restructured how a certain class of Indian professional thinks about where they live. Remote work made geography negotiable. A generation that had accepted Mumbai and Delhi as fixed coordinates began to ask whether that acceptance was necessary.

The hill stations closest to Delhi — Shimla, Mussoorie, Manali — absorbed much of this demand and, in doing so, revealed their limitations: overcrowding, declining infrastructure, seasonal road closures that test the patience of anyone trying to work from there.

Kasauli sits below all of that noise. It is 60 km from Chandigarh, which means it is 250 km from Delhi — drivable in four hours on a clear run. The hill station itself is small and quiet. The cantonment prevents the kind of commercial development that has consumed Shimla's lower reaches. The forest cover is intact.

## What we are building

IRA Estates Kasauli will be six residences, not eight. The site is smaller, the terrain more demanding, and the architectural response necessarily different from Benaulim. Where the Goa villas breathe outward — verandahs, pool decks, gardens that blur into the site — the Kasauli residences will have a more introverted logic. Large windows. Deep roof overhangs. Spaces designed for the particular pleasure of being inside while the pine forest is immediately outside.

> "At 6,000 ft, the architecture is not fighting the landscape. It is surrendering to it."

Details are being held close until closer to launch. Those who register interest will be among the first to hear.
